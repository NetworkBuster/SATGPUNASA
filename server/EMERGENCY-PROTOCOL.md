# Emergency & Basic Care Protocol — Acute Gastrointestinal Distress

Purpose: provide a concise, reproducible protocol to triage, stabilize, and escalate care for someone with acute diarrhea/vomiting and poor oral intake (example: Andrew). Use this as an on-site quick reference and checklist.

---

## 1) Immediate triage (first 60 minutes) ✅
- Assess airway, breathing, circulation (ABC). If any compromise → call emergency services immediately.
- Check these red flags (if any are present, seek urgent/emergency care):
  - Persistent **high fever** (>39°C / 102.2°F)
  - **Severe abdominal pain** or progressive pain
  - **Blood in stool** or black/tarry stool
  - **Repeated vomiting** (cannot keep fluids down)
  - **Dizziness, fainting, confusion, or very low urine output** (dehydration)
  - Signs of shock (pale/clammy, rapid weak pulse)

If one or more red-flag signs exist: call emergency services or get the person to urgent care now. While arranging transport, begin oral rehydration by small sips if tolerated.

---

## 2) Home-stabilization steps (0–6 hours) 🕒
1. Hydration first: the priority is replacing fluids and electrolytes.
   - ORS (best): Pedialyte or similar oral rehydration solution. Give frequent small sips (5–15 ml every few minutes). For adults, aim for 200–300 ml per hour initially. If vomiting, reduce to 1–2 teaspoons every 1–2 minutes and increase as tolerated.
   - If ORS not available: mix 1 L water + 6 tsp sugar + 1/2 tsp salt (homemade ORS). Avoid undiluted sports drinks or plain sugary drinks alone.
2. Food: Begin bland, easy-to-digest foods when hunger returns — BRAT (banana, rice, applesauce, toast) + clear broth. Avoid dairy, fried/greasy, spicy, and high-fiber foods initially.
3. Fever/ache: Use acetaminophen (paracetamol) as needed per dosing guidance (check weight/age). Avoid NSAIDs if dehydrated or kidney disease present.
4. Meds: Do NOT use anti-diarrheal agents (e.g., loperamide) if fever or blood in stool without medical advice. For purely non-bloody, low-fever cases in adults, loperamide may reduce stool frequency but use with caution.

---

## 3) Monitoring & documentation (every hour for first 6 hours) 📋
- Vital checks: temperature, pulse, respiratory rate, level of consciousness.
- Fluid balance: count sips, estimate urine output. Mark urine frequency and color (normal ~clear to pale).
- Stool frequency & appearance: watery, bloody, black, mucous, etc.
- Food tolerance and vomiting episodes.
- Record medications given (dose/time).

Create a short log (paper or phone note): Time | Fluid given (mL) | Vomited? | Stool | Temp | Urine | Notes

---

## 4) When to escalate (call, urgent care, or emergency) 🚨
- Call emergency services immediately if: severe abdominal pain, blood in stool, fainting, confusion, signs of shock, or unrelenting vomiting and inability to keep fluids.
- Go to urgent care within hours if: moderate dehydration (dry mouth, decreased urine), fever >38.5°C, persistent symptoms >24 hours, or concern due to age/underlying condition.
- Contact primary care if mild/moderate symptoms and improvement not seen within 48 hours.

---

## 5) Transportation & safety checklist for urgent transfer 🚗
- Bring: ID, list of medications, allergies, pre-built log of vital signs and intake, last 24-hour stool/vomit record, and any preexisting conditions.
- If dehydrated/lightheaded: move slowly, have someone accompany the patient, drive with care or call EMS if too weak.

---

## 6) Supplies checklist (immediate kit) 🧰
- Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS) x2 L or powder packs
- Bottled water
- Thermometer
- Simple carbohydrate snack (bananas, crackers)
- Acetaminophen (adult and pediatric doses) and non-prescription meds (per docs)
- Small measuring cup and spoon
- Paper log and pen

---

## 7) Communication templates 🗣️
Call/EMT script (quick):
"This is [your name]; my [relation], Andrew, age [X], has had multiple episodes of watery diarrhea since [time], has vomited [n] times today, and has not eaten. He is [mention any alarming sign: fever/dizzy/bloody stool]. We need urgent evaluation." 

Message to on-call clinician (text/email):
Subject: Acute diarrhea/vomiting — short summary
Body: Name/Age; Onset and timeline; Episodes of vomiting (count), stool (count/appearance), oral intake (none), vitals (if known), existing conditions/meds, red flags present (yes/no). Request: telehealth evaluation or urgent care instructions.

---

## 8) Basic nursing care protocol (for caregivers) 🩺
- Ensure comfortable position; small frequent sips; frequent reassessment.
- Offer rehydration with small volumes after each vomit (wait 10–20 minutes), then resume slowly.
- Keep environment calm and safe; minimize strong odors.
- If caring for an infant/child, follow pediatric-specific ORS dosing and seek pediatric care early.

---

## 9) Post-acute follow-up & recovery guidance (0–14 days) ✅
- If improved in 24–48 hours: continue gradual diet, maintain hydration, return to normal activity as tolerated.
- If not improved or symptoms recur: schedule primary care or stool testing as advised by clinician.

---

## 10) Quick printable one-line checklist (copy this on a note) 📝
1) Check ABCs, call EMS if airway/breathing/circulation compromise
2) Give ORS in small sips, monitor urine and mental status
3) No anti-diarrhea if fever or blood present
4) Watch for red flags → urgent care/emergency
